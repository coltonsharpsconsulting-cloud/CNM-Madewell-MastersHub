DMP #3 — Digital Storytelling
Chasity N. Madewell

MY DIGITAL STORY
"Ruby's Big Delivery: A Red Blood Cell's Journey Through the Body" — a six-scene
animated digital story that follows Ruby, a red blood cell, on one full loop of the
circulatory system: the right heart, the lungs (where she trades carbon dioxide for
oxygen and turns from violet to bright red), the powerful left heart, the branching
arteries, a capillary delivery at a hard-working muscle, and the trip home through the
veins. Each scene pairs the story with a short "Science note" so it works as a real
lesson, not just a cartoon.

HOW TO VIEW IT
Open the attached file "index.html" in any web browser (Chrome works best), then press
"Begin the Journey." Use the Next/Back buttons or the arrow keys to move through the six
scenes. Turn sound ON for the heartbeat, the "whoosh" of the pumping heart, and a
read-aloud narration of each page. Watch Ruby's color change as she picks up and drops
off oxygen, and watch the oxygen meter rise and fall with her.

EXPLORING THE PLATFORMS (the fun part)
Since the focus of this project was exploring digital storytelling tools, I test-drove
several from the list before deciding how to build:
- Book Creator — clean and genuinely easy; great for a printable/flip e-book, but the
  free version caps you at a few books and the animation is limited.
- Adobe Express (Spark) — beautiful templates and slick video stories, but the best
  motion and voiceover features sit behind the paid tier.
- Storyboard That — perfect for a quick comic-strip storyboard; I actually used it to
  rough out Ruby's six "panels" before building.
- Storybird & Animoto — lovely for art-first and photo/video stories respectively, but
  harder to bend to a science topic with custom characters.

I decided to build my story as a lightweight, self-contained web app (HTML, CSS, and
JavaScript are themselves a digital storytelling platform) so I could fully control the
animation, the color change that teaches gas exchange, the synced heartbeat sound, and
the read-aloud narration — with no account or paywall, and nothing for a student to
install.

WHY THIS ONE
I teach 7th-grade life science, so I built something I can actually use Monday. Ruby's
journey doubles as a multimodal digital text for our body-systems unit, and making it
was the best way to feel — as a creator, not just a viewer — exactly what this module's
readings argue: that digital storytelling is about choices in image, motion, sound, and
text working together to make meaning. It was, as promised, fun.

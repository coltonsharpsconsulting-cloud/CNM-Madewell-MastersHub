Assessment Suite — Cells: The Building Blocks of Life

Chasity N. Madewell · 7th-Grade Life Science · American Leadership Academy

Aligned to: AZ Science Standard 7.L1U1.6 · NGSS MS-LS1-1, MS-LS1-2, MS-LS1-3 — and the unit objectives from my SLO 2 Backward Design.
Assessments Diagnostic Formative Summative Authentic

This suite contains four aligned assessments for my three-week cells unit — a diagnostic to surface prior knowledge and misconceptions, a formative check to drive in-the-moment intervention, a summative assessment of the unit objectives, and an authentic performance task. Each is designed around the same essential idea the unit builds toward — structure determines function — and each includes differentiation tied to the diverse learners profiled in my SLO 1 classroom snapshot.

Privacy note: Any student references (e.g., the focus student “Mateo”) are de-identified composites from my SLO 1 profile, used to show how each assessment is differentiated. No real student is identifiable.
Diagnostic

“What Do You Already Know About Cells?”

When: Day 1, before instruction. Time: ~15 min. Use: Not graded for accuracy — it groups students and targets reteaching. Results set my flexible groups and flag misconceptions to confront in Lessons 1–2.

Measures readiness for: MS-LS1-1 (living things are made of cells) and the unit’s core vocabulary, before any of it is taught.

Part A · Quick Probes (circle one)

1. The smallest unit that is still considered “living” is the…

  • atom
  • cell
  • organ
  • molecule

2. Which of these is made of cells?

  • a tree
  • a fish
  • you
  • all of these

3. Agree or Disagree, then say why in one sentence: “Only animals are made of cells.”

I ___ (agree / disagree) because ____________________.

4. Agree or Disagree, then say why: “All cells come from other cells that already exist.”

I ___ (agree / disagree) because ____________________.

Part B · Draw & Label (no words needed to start)

In the box, draw what you think a cell looks like. Label any parts you already know the names of. If you’ve seen one under a microscope, draw what you remember.

[ drawing space ]

Part C · K–W (what I Know / what I Want to know)

Something I already KNOW about cellsSomething I WANT to figure out

How I use the results

  • Misconception flags: Items 3–4 target the two most common 7th-grade misconceptions (plants/“non-animals” aren’t cellular; spontaneous generation). Disagreement patterns tell me what to confront directly in the cell-theory lesson.
  • Grouping: Part B drawings sort quickly into emerging / developing / secure prior schema, which seeds my Day 2–3 flexible groups.
  • No grade: Scored only as complete/incomplete so students answer honestly.

Differentiation

Multiple means of representation/expression (UDL)
The “draw a cell” task lets students who read below grade level — including my focus student Mateo — show prior knowledge without a reading bottleneck. Probes can be read aloud or run via text-to-speech.
English Learners (SIOP)
Key terms appear with a small picture glossary and Spanish cognates (cell / célula); oral or single-word responses are accepted on Parts A and C.
IEP/504 & ADHD
Short (15 min), low-stakes, chunked into three small parts; extended time and a quiet-start option available.
Gifted / secure-prior-knowledge
Stretch prompt: “Name one question scientists still can’t fully answer about cells.”
Formative

Organelle Function Check + Self-Rating

When: Mid-unit (end of Lesson 3, “Organelles: Structure & Function”). Time: ~10 min exit ticket. Use: Real-time check that drives next-day small-group reteach and confirms readiness to move to “levels of organization.”

Measures progress toward: MS-LS1-2 (parts of a cell contribute to its function) and the “structure determines function” essential question.

Part 1 · Match (word bank provided)

Word bank: nucleus · mitochondria · chloroplast · cell membrane · cell wall · ribosome

  1. Controls the cell and stores DNA → __________
  2. Releases energy from food (“powerhouse”) → __________
  3. Captures sunlight to make food (plants) → __________
  4. Controls what enters and leaves the cell → __________
  5. Makes proteins → __________

Part 2 · Structure → Function (CER sentence frame)

Pick one organelle. Explain how its structure helps it do its job.

The  (organelle)  helps the cell by  (function) . I know this because its structure  (evidence) .

Part 3 · “Cell City” quick check + confidence

If the cell were a city, the mitochondria would be the ____________ because ____________.

My confidence right now (circle one): 🟢 I’ve got it · 🟡 almost · 🔴 I need another look

Answer key & response plan

Part 1: 1 nucleus · 2 mitochondria · 3 chloroplast · 4 cell membrane · 5 ribosome. (Cell wall is the distractor — used to check the plant/animal distinction.)

  • 🔴/🟡 students → next-day reteach table with the Frayer cards and a hands-on organelle sort while 🟢 students start the levels-of-organization extension.
  • Part 2 is scored for reasoning, not vocabulary polish — a correct structure→function link earns full credit even with simple wording.

Differentiation

Scaffolded supports (HLP 15)
Word bank + CER sentence frame remove the recall and syntax load so students are graded on science reasoning. Frames are faded later in the unit.
Mateo & below-grade readers
May give Part 2 as a 20-second oral recording or by annotating an organelle diagram instead of writing.
English Learners
Frayer visuals and cognates remain posted; partner talk precedes the written response (comprehensible input + rehearsal).
Gifted
Stretch: “Choose two organelles and explain how they depend on each other.”
Metacognition (HLP 14)
The 🟢🟡🔴 self-rating builds self-monitoring and gives me instant grouping data.
Summative

End-of-Unit Cells Assessment

When: End of unit (after Lesson 5). Time: ~45 min. Use: Measures mastery of all unit objectives; the data identify who needs intervention before the next unit (body systems).

Measures: AZ 7.L1U1.6 · MS-LS1-1, -2, -3 — organelle function, plant/animal & prokaryote/eukaryote, microscope & scale, levels of organization, and a Claim–Evidence–Reasoning explanation.

Section 1 · Organelles (multiple choice)

1. Which organelle is found in plant cells but not animal cells and captures light energy?

  • mitochondria
  • chloroplast
  • nucleus
  • ribosome

2. A cell needs to make a lot of protein. Which organelle should it have many of?

  • vacuoles
  • ribosomes
  • cell walls
  • chloroplasts

Section 2 · Compare & Contrast

3. Give one difference and one similarity between a prokaryotic and a eukaryotic cell.

4. Name two structures a plant cell has that an animal cell does not.

Section 3 · Microscope & Scale (math integration)

5. A microscope’s eyepiece (ocular) lens is 10× and the objective lens is 40×. What is the total magnification? Show your work.

Section 4 · Levels of Organization

6. Put these in order from smallest to largest: organ, cell, organism, tissue, organ system.

7. Explain in one sentence how a single muscle cell is related to the muscular system.

Section 5 · Constructed Response (Claim–Evidence–Reasoning)

8. Answer the essential question: “How does a cell’s structure determine its function?” Use one specific organelle as your example.

Claim: ______. Evidence: The structure of the ______ is ______. Reasoning: This structure lets the cell ______, which shows that structure determines function.

Answer key & CER rubric

1 B · 2 B · 3 e.g., difference: prokaryotes lack a nucleus / membrane-bound organelles; similarity: both have a cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, DNA · 4 cell wall & chloroplast (also accept large central vacuole) · 5 10 × 40 = 400× · 6 cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism · 7 muscle cells form muscle tissue, which forms the organs/organ system that move the body.

CER level (Item 8)Descriptor
4 — MasteryAccurate claim; specific structural evidence for the chosen organelle; reasoning explicitly links structure → function.
3 — ProficientCorrect claim & evidence; reasoning present but general.
2 — DevelopingClaim correct; evidence vague or reasoning missing.
1 — BeginningRestates the question or lists a fact without linking structure to function.

Differentiation (access without lowering the target)

Representation
Read-aloud / text-to-speech for the whole test; a posted picture-glossary of organelles; key academic verbs (“compare,” “explain”) defined in the margin.
Expression (Mateo & SLD-reading students)
Item 8 may be completed as a recorded oral CER or a labeled diagram with captions — the science target (structure→function reasoning) is held constant; only the mode of demonstration changes.
English Learners
Bilingual glossary permitted; sentence frame on Item 8 supplies the academic-language structure.
IEP/504
Extended time, small-group/quiet setting, and reduced-distractor versions of Section 1 as specified in student plans.
Gifted
Optional challenge: “Predict what happens to the whole organism if one organelle stops working — trace the effect up the levels of organization.”
Authentic

“Design-a-Cell” Analogy Model & Presentation

When: Unit synthesis (Lesson 5), alongside the summative. Format: Performance task with an authentic audience. Use: Students apply the unit’s big idea to a real-world system of their choice and explain it — assessment as learning.

Measures: MS-LS1-2 & MS-LS1-3 (a cell as a system of interacting parts) through application, modeling, and oral/visual communication — the deepest evidence of transfer.

The Task

You are a “systems designer.” Choose a real system you know well — a city, factory, school, theme park, restaurant, or sports team — and build a model or labeled diagram that shows how that system is just like a cell. Then present it (live, or as a 2–3 minute recorded video) explaining:

  1. Which part of your system represents each major organelle, and why (match the job, not just the name).
  2. How the parts work together as a system — what happens to the whole if one part fails.
  3. One way your analogy is not perfect (where the comparison breaks down).

Audience: presented to the class (and shared with a 6th-grade class as “cell experts”).

Differentiation

Interest & choice (Tomlinson)
Students choose the system (sports, gaming, city, restaurant…), which raises engagement and lets cultural and personal background become an asset.
Multiple means of expression
Model can be physical, drawn, digital (slides), or a diorama; the explanation can be live or recorded — ideal for Mateo, who reasons strongly orally and draws excellent diagrams.
English Learners
Bilingual labels welcomed; the planning organizer and a rehearsal with a partner provide language support before presenting.
IEP/504 & ADHD
Organizer chunks the task; defined group roles; checkpoints across two days rather than one open-ended block.
Gifted
Extension: model a disease or malfunction (e.g., a mitochondria “power outage”) and trace the consequences up the levels of organization.

Summative

End-of-Unit Cells Assessment

When: End of unit (after Lesson 5). Time: ~45 min. Use: Measures mastery of all unit objectives; the data identify who needs intervention before the next unit (body systems).

Measures: AZ 7.L1U1.6 · MS-LS1-1, -2, -3 — organelle function, plant/animal & prokaryote/eukaryote, microscope & scale, levels of organization, and a Claim–Evidence–Reasoning explanation.

Section 1 · Organelles (multiple choice)

1. Which organelle is found in plant cells but not animal cells and captures light energy?

  • mitochondria
  • chloroplast
  • nucleus
  • ribosome

2. A cell needs to make a lot of protein. Which organelle should it have many of?

  • vacuoles
  • ribosomes
  • cell walls
  • chloroplasts

Section 2 · Compare & Contrast

3. Give one difference and one similarity between a prokaryotic and a eukaryotic cell.

4. Name two structures a plant cell has that an animal cell does not.

Section 3 · Microscope & Scale (math integration)

5. A microscope’s eyepiece (ocular) lens is 10× and the objective lens is 40×. What is the total magnification? Show your work.

Section 4 · Levels of Organization

6. Put these in order from smallest to largest: organ, cell, organism, tissue, organ system.

7. Explain in one sentence how a single muscle cell is related to the muscular system.

Section 5 · Constructed Response (Claim–Evidence–Reasoning)

8. Answer the essential question: “How does a cell’s structure determine its function?” Use one specific organelle as your example.

Claim: ______. Evidence: The structure of the ______ is ______. Reasoning: This structure lets the cell ______, which shows that structure determines function.

Answer key & CER rubric

1 B · 2 B · 3 e.g., difference: prokaryotes lack a nucleus / membrane-bound organelles; similarity: both have a cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, DNA · 4 cell wall & chloroplast (also accept large central vacuole) · 5 10 × 40 = 400× · 6 cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism · 7 muscle cells form muscle tissue, which forms the organs/organ system that move the body.

CER level (Item 8)Descriptor
4 — MasteryAccurate claim; specific structural evidence for the chosen organelle; reasoning explicitly links structure → function.
3 — ProficientCorrect claim & evidence; reasoning present but general.
2 — DevelopingClaim correct; evidence vague or reasoning missing.
1 — BeginningRestates the question or lists a fact without linking structure to function.

Differentiation (access without lowering the target)

Representation
Read-aloud / text-to-speech for the whole test; a posted picture-glossary of organelles; key academic verbs (“compare,” “explain”) defined in the margin.
Expression (Mateo & SLD-reading students)
Item 8 may be completed as a recorded oral CER or a labeled diagram with captions — the science target (structure→function reasoning) is held constant; only the mode of demonstration changes.
English Learners
Bilingual glossary permitted; sentence frame on Item 8 supplies the academic-language structure.
IEP/504
Extended time, small-group/quiet setting, and reduced-distractor versions of Section 1 as specified in student plans.
Gifted
Optional challenge: “Predict what happens to the whole organism if one organelle stops working — trace the effect up the levels of organization.”
